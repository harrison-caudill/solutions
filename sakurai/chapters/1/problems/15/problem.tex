\begin{question}

A beam of spin $\frac 1 2$ atoms goes through a series of
Stern–Gerlach type measurements as follows.

\begin{enumerate}[label=\alph*.]

\item The first measurement accepts $S_z = \frac{\hbar}{2}$ atoms and
  rejects $S_z = -\frac{\hbar}{2}$ atoms.

\item The second measurement accepts $S_n = \frac{\hbar}{2}$ atoms and
  rejects $S_n = -\frac{\hbar}{2}$, where $S_n$ is the eigenvalue of
  the operator $\vectorbold{S} \cdot \vectorbold{\hat{n}}$, with
  $\vectorbold{\hat{n}}$ making an angle $\beta$ in the xz-plane with
  respect to the z-axis.

\item The third measurement accepts $S_z = -\frac{\hbar}{2}$ atoms and
  rejects $S_z = \frac{\hbar}{2}$ atoms.

\end{enumerate}

\begin{questionpart}
  What is the intensity of the final $S_z = -\frac{\hbar}{2}$ beam
  when the $S_z = \frac{\hbar}{2}$ beam surviving the first
  measurement is normalized to unity?
\end{questionpart}

\begin{questionpart}
 How must we orient the second measuring apparatus if we are to
 maximize the intensity of the final $S_z = \frac{\hbar}{2}$ beam?
\end{questionpart}

\end{question}

\begin{purpose}
  This one looks like basic practice with how state collapses across
  observations and how that affects probabilities.  Interestingly, it
  also sets us up to better understand the classical experiment where
  3 polarizing filters will work where 2 will not and also how LCD
  displays work.
\end{purpose}

\begin{answer}

  \begin{answerpart}{Infant Mortality}
    For simplicity, let's put up a quick diagram of the pipeline of
    experiments:

    \begin{center}
      \begin{figure}
        \inlabel{pipeline}
        \includegraphics[width=3in]{pipeline.png}
        \caption{Phase a}
      \end{figure}
    \end{center}

    Let's start with with intuition about the physics of the problem
    and see where it leads, then we can try to grind it out and
    compare results.  We can say that the $a \rightarrow b$ beam is
    homongenously in the $\ket{z+}$ state as only atoms with an
    observed angular momentum in the $z$ axis of $\frac{\hbar}{2}$
    will pass, and others shall fall to the depths of Moria.

    Next, we consider our boundaries: at $\beta = 0$ the full beam
    survives and turned to $\beta = \frac{\pi}{2}$ only half the beam
    survives.  Finally, if $\beta = -\pi$, then all is lost.  Any
    atoms incident upon box $b$ can be said to be in one of two
    states: $\ket{n+}$ and $\ket{n-}$.  Of those lil' guys, any of
    them in the $\ket{n+}$ will pass (so, 100\%).  Any in the
    $\ket{n-}$ will fall (so 0\%). So our task, then, would be to find
    out what fraction ($p$) is $\ket{n+}$ and the deduce what fraction
    ($q = 1 - p$) is not.

    So, we can pretty easily see that we're going to have a
    coefficient of a $\sin$ or $\cos$ or something to let us know what
    fraction will pass.  We can also pretty easily enumerate a few
    specific cases:

    \begin{equation}
      \begin{split}
        p(\beta=0) &= 1\\
        p(\beta=\frac{\pi}{2}) &= \frac{1}{2}\\
        p(\beta=\pi) &= 0\\
        p(\beta=\frac{3\pi}{2}) &= \frac{1}{2}\\
      \end{split}
    \end{equation}

    Remembering, also, that probabilities are proportional to the
    square of the amplitude, we're probably looking for something of
    the form:

    \begin{equation}
      p(\beta) = c_1 + c_2\cos^2{\beta}
    \end{equation}

    Solving for some initial conditions, and we can postulate a
    solution:
    
    \begin{equation}
      P(\beta) = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{\cos{\beta}}{2}
    \end{equation}

    The problem with this solution is that it's a $\cos$ instead of
    something squared like we would expect for a probability vs a wave
    amplitude.  Thankfully, we have the half-angle formulas to help us
    out here:

    \begin{equation}
      \begin{alignedat}{2}
        & & \cos{\frac \alpha 2} &= \pm \sqrt{\frac{1 + \cos{\alpha}}{2}}\\
        \implies \quad & & \cos^2{\frac \beta 2} &= \frac{1}{2} + \frac{\cos{\beta}}{2}\\
      \end{alignedat}
    \end{equation}
    
    That leaves us with our final ansatz'd solution of:
    \begin{equation}
      P(\beta) = \cos^2{\frac \beta 2}
    \end{equation}
    
    Evaluating our new expression at the test points:
    \begin{equation}
      \begin{split}
        p(\beta=0) &= 1\qed\\
        p(\beta=\frac{\pi}{2}) &= \frac{1}{2}\qed\\
        p(\beta=\pi) &= 0\qed\\
        p(\beta=\frac{3\pi}{2}) &= \frac{1}{2}\qed\\
      \end{split}
    \end{equation}

    Using the same approach, we estimate the beam intensity out of
    gate $c$ (since it's symmetric, just showing $q$ instead of $p$
    since the last gate passes $\ket{x-}$):

    \begin{equation}
      \inlabel{touchyFeely}
      \begin{split}
        P_{b \rightarrow} &= \cos^2{\frac \beta 2}\\
        P_{c \rightarrow} &= \cos^2{\frac \beta 2} (1 - \cos^2{\frac \beta 2})\\
        &= \cos^2{\frac \beta 2} \sin^2{\frac \beta 2}\\
        &= \left(\frac{\sin{\beta}}{2}\right)^2\\
        &= \frac{\sin^2{\beta}}{4}\\
      \end{split}
    \end{equation}
    
    Evaluating our new expression at the test points:
    \begin{equation}
      \begin{split}
        p(\beta=0) &= 0\qed\\
        p(\beta=\frac{\pi}{2}) &= \frac{1}{4}\qed\\
        p(\beta=\pi) &= 0\qed\\
        p(\beta=\frac{3\pi}{2}) &= \frac{1}{4}\qed\\
      \end{split}
    \end{equation}

    This is about what we would expect.  If you're either aligned with
    or against the first or last gate, then nothing gets through.  If
    you're aligned exactly in between, then you decimate by $\frac 1
    2$ each time with a final beam intensity of $\frac 1 4$. So, our
    test-points seem to work, but that's not the same as being
    correct, so let's grind it out.

    We start by recalling equation \ref{1.14:ket} which we will use to
    construct our $\Lambda_{n+}$ operator:

    \begin{equation}
      \begin{split}
        \Lambda_{n+}
        &= \dyad{n+}\\
        &= \left(\cos{\frac{\beta}{2}} \ket{+}
        + \sin{\frac{\beta}{2}} \ket{-}\right)
        \left(\cos{\frac{\beta}{2}} \bra{+}
        + \sin{\frac{\beta}{2}} \bra{-}\right)
      \end{split}
    \end{equation}

    Acting that projection operator onto the incoming $\ket{+}$
    state, we find that the output of the box is:

    \begin{equation}
      \begin{split}
        \Lambda_{n+} \ket{+}
        &= \dyad{n+}\ket{+}\\
        &= \left(\cos{\frac{\beta}{2}} \ket{+}
        + \sin{\frac{\beta}{2}} \ket{-}\right)
        \left(\cos{\frac{\beta}{2}} \bra{+}
        + \sin{\frac{\beta}{2}} \bra{-}\right)\ket{+}\\
        &= \left(\cos{\frac{\beta}{2}} \ket{+}
        + \sin{\frac{\beta}{2}} \ket{-}\right)
        \cos{\frac{\beta}{2}}\braket{+}\\
        &= \cos{\frac{\beta}{2}}\left(\cos{\frac{\beta}{2}} \ket{+}
        + \sin{\frac{\beta}{2}} \ket{-}\right)\\
        &= \cos{\frac{\beta}{2}}\ket{n+}
      \end{split}
    \end{equation}

    Taking the probability, we find that our probability is exactly
    what we intuited earlier in equation \inref{touchyFeely}, coming
    out to:

    \begin{equation}
      \begin{split}
        P_{b \rightarrow}
        &= \cos{\frac{\beta}{2}}\bra{n+}\cos{\frac{\beta}{2}}\ket{n+}\\
        &= \cos^2{\frac{\beta}{2}}\braket{n+}\\
        &= \cos^2{\frac{\beta}{2}}\\
      \end{split}
    \end{equation}

    We then apply the $\Lambda_{-}$ to the resulting output state to
    determine the final probability and we get:

    \begin{equation}
      \begin{split}
        \Lambda_{-}\cos{\frac{\beta}{2}}\ket{n+}
        &= \dyad{-}\cos{\frac{\beta}{2}}
        \left(\cos{\frac{\beta}{2}} \ket{+}
        + \sin{\frac{\beta}{2}} \ket{-}\right)\\
        &= \cos{\frac{\beta}{2}}\sin{\frac{\beta}{2}}\ket{-}\\
      \end{split}
    \end{equation}

    Once again, taking the inner product to find the probability, we
    find that grinding it out produces exactly the same result as we
    found above through intuition:

    \begin{equation}
      \begin{split}
        P_{c \rightarrow}
        &= \cos{\frac{\beta}{2}}\sin{\frac{\beta}{2}}\bra{-}\cos{\frac{\beta}{2}}\sin{\frac{\beta}{2}}\ket{-}\\
        &= \cos^2{\frac{\beta}{2}}\sin^2{\frac{\beta}{2}}\braket{-}\\
        &= \cos^2{\frac{\beta}{2}}\sin^2{\frac{\beta}{2}}\\
        &= \left(\frac{\sin{\beta}}{2}\right)^2\\
        &= \frac{\sin^2{\beta}}{4}\qed\\
      \end{split}
    \end{equation}

    So we see that our intuition beautifully matches the
    grinding-it-out approach.
    
  \end{answerpart}

  \begin{answerpart}{Probabilitis Maximus}
    Just like with LCD screens, our output probability is maximized
    when our intermediate filter splits the difference between the
    first and the last (just look at the equation for the probability
    and you see that it's maximized at $\frac{\pi}{2}$ and
    $\frac{3\pi}{2}$).
  \end{answerpart}

\end{answer}
