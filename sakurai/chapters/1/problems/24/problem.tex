\begin{question}
  Estimate the rough order of magnitude of the length of time that an
  ice pick can be balanced on its point if the only limitation is that
  set by the Heisenberg uncertainty principle. Assume that the point
  is sharp and that the point and the surface on which it rests are
  hard. You may make approximations which do not alter the general
  order of magnitude of the result. Assume reasonable values for the
  dimensions and weight of the ice pick. Obtain an approximate
  numerical result and express it \emph{in seconds}.
\end{question}

\begin{purpose}
  This is a classical problem in high-school physics: connect the
  uncertainty relation to the real-world.  We can also see this with
  things like entanglement in macro-scale systems (I think they
  mentioned a pendulum somewhere in the book) and Bose condensates.

  There are several concepts which need to come together for this
  problem

  \begin{itemize}
  \item Application of the $X/P$ uncertainty relation.
  \item Application of the $E/t$ uncertainty relation.
  \item Taking expectation values using wave functions.
  \item The Wigner function.
  \item Wave function collapse after observations.
  \item Mass energy and momentum implications from special relativity.
  \end{itemize}

  We're going to solve the problem using a few different approaches:
  
  \begin{questionpart}
    The most straight-forward normal way of doing this problem is to
    assume a wave function for the position, determine the \acp{pdf}
    for $P$ and $X$ and integrate against the fall-time as a function
    of those variables.  True to form, things are out of order here.
  \end{questionpart}

  \begin{questionpart}
    The next fun way to solve the problem is to leverage the $E/t$
    uncertainty relation to determine the amount of rotational kinetic
    energy in the system.  Using the time during the observation of
    energy, we then reverse-engineer the presumed initial conditions
    and compute the expecation value just as we did in the previous
    part.
  \end{questionpart}

  \begin{questionpart}
    Finally, we use the $E/t$ uncertainty relation to, for this
    problem, reconstruct the $X/P$ uncertainty relation at which point
    the initial solution again applies.
  \end{questionpart}

  Note that I actually solved this problem out of order.  Problem
  \ref{1.36:theQuestion} involves proving one of the key relations for
  the solution to this problem so I decided to solve that one first.
  
\end{purpose}

  \begin{answerpart}{The ``normal'' way}

    I mean...I \emph{assume} this is the normal way at least.  Like
    the old joke about Werner Heisenberg getting pulled over by the
    cops goes, we can't know our position and momentum at the same
    time.  We just went to great pains to prove that if $\vec{a}^{*}
    \cdot \vec{a} \ge 0$ then the uncertainty relation is a thing.  So
    let's run with that.  Starting with our uncertainty relation:

    \begin{equation}
      \tag{1.146}
      \dispersion{A}\dispersion{B} \ge \frac{1}{4} \abs{\expval{[A, B]}}^2
    \end{equation}

    So swapping out those operators to be the ones we're interested in:
  
    \begin{equation}
      \dispersion{x}\dispersion{p} \ge \frac{1}{4} \abs{\expval{[x, p]}}^2
    \end{equation}

    Next, it's worth looking at our momentum operator:
    \begin{equation}
      \hat{p} \approx -\hbar^2\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x'^2}
    \end{equation}

    So, if we pull in a few utility equations:

    \begin{equation}
      \tag{1.98}
      \expval{A} \equiv \mel{\alpha}{A}{\alpha}
    \end{equation}

    \begin{equation}
      \tag{1.143}
      \Delta A \equiv A - \expval{A}
    \end{equation}
    
    \begin{equation}
      \tag{1.144}
      \dispersion{A}
      = \expval{(A^2 - 2A\expval{A} + \expval{A}^2)}
      = \expval{A^2} - \expval{A}^2
    \end{equation}

    \begin{equation}
      \tag{1.251}
      \inlabel{exp:p}
      \begin{split}
        \mel{\beta}{p}{\alpha}
        &= \int_0^a dx' \braket{\beta}{x'}
        \left(
        -i \hbar \pdv{}{x'} \braket{x'}{a}
        \right)\\
        &= \int_0^a dx'
        \psi_{\beta}^{*}(x')
        \operatorP{x'}
        \psi_{\alpha}(x')\\
      \end{split}
    \end{equation}

    So, we can pretty quickly see that all of our equations of
    interest for computing the uncertainty ultimately just come down
    to the state-ket $\ket{\psi}$:

    Putting it all together, we find that:

    \begin{equation}
      \inlabel{handy}
      \begin{alignedat}{2}
        \expval{x}^2 &= \mel{\psi}{x}{\psi}^2 &
        &= \left( \int dx \psi^{*}(x) x \psi(x) \right)^2
        \\
        \expval{x^2} &= \mel{\psi}{x^2}{\psi} &
        &= \int dx \psi^{*}(x) x^2 \psi(x)
        \\
        \expval{p}^2 &= \mel{\psi}{p}{\psi}^2 &
        &= \left( \int dx \psi^{*}(x) \left(-i \hbar\frac{\partial}{\partial x} \right) \psi(x) \right)^2
        \\
        \expval{p^2} &= \mel{\psi}{p^2}{\psi} &
        &= \int dx \psi^{*}(x) \left(-\hbar^2\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x'^2} \right) \psi(x)
        \\
      \end{alignedat}
    \end{equation}

    So, just as we were taught in wave mechanics, you know Saul, you
    know em' all...no wait, that isn't it...you know $\psi$ you know
    it all...less catchy, more correct.  Effectively that means that

    \begin{equation}
      \dispersion{x}\dispersion{p} = f(\psi(x))
    \end{equation}

    Since the uncertainty relation must hold true but also be minimized,
    we can go one step further and say that:
    
    \begin{equation}
      \begin{split}
        \inlabel{coreConjecture}
        \dispersion{x}\dispersion{p}
        &= f(\psi(x))\\
        &= \frac{1}{4} \abs{\expval{[x, p]}}^2
      \end{split}
    \end{equation}

    So, if we have equality, then it means that the only wave
    functions we're going to accept are those which meet that
    equality.  Here, we're going to make an assumption.  Since the
    whole concept is ``place the ice pick and make it reeeeeeally
    steady'' then we're going to assume that placement of the ice-pick
    is a gaussian with respect to the top of the ice pick (don't
    worry, we'll multiply by 2 there to account for uncertainty of the
    top and bottom).  It's also worth noting that Gaussian wave
    packets are known to minimize uncertainty.
  
    So, that gives us an equation with one uknown: $\sigma$ (or at
    least its spirit animal).  So, let's assume we already know sigma
    (we'll solve for it later).  What do we do with $\sigma$?  Well,
    don't know where the ice-pick is initially \emph{or} what it's
    initial momentum is...there's that whole fundamental
    indeterminability thing going on.  But what we \emph{do} know is
    the \ac{pdf} for position which also determines the \ac{pdf} for
    the momentum.  So, what we can do with \acp{pdf} is to determine
    an \emph{expectation value}.  OK...what are we taking the
    expectation value of?  Well, that we get from the problem setup:
    the fall time.

    Armed with the knowledge of (the construction of) the \ac{lhs} of
    our equation, we now proceed to figuring out the \ac{rhs}.  If
    we're going to take an expectation value of the fall time, then
    we're expecting something like this:

    \begin{equation}
      \inlabel{guidingIntegral}
      <t> = \int \int dx dp P(x)P(p)f(x, p)
    \end{equation}

    If we're going to evalute that, we're going to need to know how to
    compute the probability of the position, and momentum.  \S 1.7.3
    in the book discusses this exact issue and formulates the position
    and momentum space wave functions (which happen to be fourier
    transforms of one another...iiinteresting...kinda makes you wonder
    about Shor's algorithm doesn't it).

    Starting with the statistical interpretation from the text just
    after eq 1.256, ``the probability that a measurement of $p$ gives
    eigenvalue $p'$ within a narrow interval $dp'$ is
    $\abs{\braket{p'}{\alpha}}^2dp'$.  It is customary to call
    $\braket{p'}{\alpha}$ the \textbf{momentum-space wave function};
    the notation $\phi_{\alpha}(p')$ is often used''.  So, if we have
    an expression for $\phi_{\alpha}(p')$ then we can find the
    probability:

    \begin{equation}
      P(p = p' \pm dp'/2) = \abs{\phi_{\alpha}(p')}^2dp'
    \end{equation}

    Later, Sakurai provides us with that exact equation:
    
    \begin{equation}
      \tag{1.266a}
      \psi_{\alpha}(x')
      = \left[ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}} \right]
      \int dp' \exp(\frac{ip'x'}{\hbar})\phi_{\alpha}(p')
    \end{equation}

    \begin{equation}
      \tag{1.266b}
      \phi_{\alpha}(p')
      = \left[ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}} \right]
      \int dp' \exp(\frac{-ip'x'}{\hbar})\psi_{\alpha}(x')
    \end{equation}

    So, we can already obtain the position-space variant more simply
    with

    \begin{equation}
      P(x') = \abs{\braket{\alpha}{x'}}^2
    \end{equation}

    This gives us expressions for the probabilities for both the $x$
    and $p$ values for us to take our integral in equation
    \ref{guidingIntegral}.  That brings us to the fall-time, and the
    issue of translation vs rotation.

    Let's say that you have a very very good machine which places your
    ice-pick in exactly the right place, but it may jerk at the last
    possible moment and impart momentum.  Let's further suppose that
    when it does so, it dosn't twist the ice-pick at all, but rather
    causes it to glide on the infinite field of pefectly flat hyle
    without any resistances.  Let's further assume that there are no
    relativistic effects with which to contend (I mean...why not
    right?  we're assuming we have Hyle, so Aristotle would be proud
    already).  If the machine imparts no rotation, only translation
    then the uncertainty relation is satisfied and yet it never falls
    right?  Well...no...as it turns out.  The easy way to prove this
    to yourself is to put yourself into the frame of reference of the
    tip of the ice-pick.  In that frame of reference, you're
    describing a system in which the uncertainties in position and
    momentum are both 0.  Because we're trying to minimize the
    uncertainty product, it also means that we get to assume that all
    of that momentum uncertainty goes into rotational momentum, and we
    also get to assume that all of that positional uncertainty is
    asymmetric between the top and the bottom of the ice-pick.  In
    other words, because we're minimizing it, we get to assume that
    all the uncertainty goes into making it fall faster.

    So, we'll incorporate our $\Delta p$ and $\Delta x$ as follows:

    \begin{equation}
      \begin{split}
        \omega_0 &= \frac{p_0}{I}\\
        \theta_0 &\approx x_0\\
      \end{split}
    \end{equation}

    So that should be all of the pieces necessary to show the
    expectation value for the fall time of an ideal and ideally-placed
    ice-pick on an infinitely flat frictionless physics-plane.

  \end{answerpart}

  \begin{answerpart}{How Much Time/Energy Do I Have?}
    We're also going to approach the problem from a different
    uncertainty relation: the time/energy uncertainty.  This one is
    outside the scope of chapter 1...it's just fun...

    \begin{equation}
      E = \frac{1}{2} I \omega^2
    \end{equation}

    Using the same relativity logic as above, we can show that a
    0-energy state (disallowed for other reasons) would be required
    for it to not fall.  Knowing its rotational kinetic energy and a
    time-window in which that (average) energy is true implies the
    ability to compute not only the current $\omega$ but also the
    initial conditions to satisfy the constraints of the fall-time
    equation given the time over which the observation is made.
    
  \end{answerpart}
