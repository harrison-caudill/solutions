\begin{question}

A spin $\frac 1 2$ system is known to be in an eigenstate of
$\vectorbold{S}\cdot\vectorbold{\hat{n}}$ with eigenvalue $\hbar/2$,
where $\vectorbold{\hat{n}}$ is a unit vector lying in the xz-plane
that makes an angle $\gamma$ with the positive z-axis.

\begin{questionpart}
  Suppose $S_x$ is measured. What is the probability of getting $+\hbar/2$?
\end{questionpart}

\begin{questionpart}
  Evaluate the dispersion in $S_x$, that is
  \begin{equation*}
    \expval{(S_x - \expval{S_x})^2}
  \end{equation*}
\end{questionpart}

(For your own peace of mind check your answers for the special cases
$\gamma = 0\text{, }\pi/2\text{, and }\pi.$)
\end{question}

\begin{purpose}
  Learn how to apply our result from problem \ref{1.11:theQuestion}.
  I'm guessing that he's also trying to help us understand the need
  for the upcoming discussions of the Bloch Sphere.
\end{purpose}

\begin{answer}

  \begin{answerpart}{Probability}
    Let's start by putting down a few equations we get from the
    problem definition.  We know that $\vectorbold{\hat{n}}$ \emph{is}
    in the $xz$ plane, which means that it is \emph{not} at all on the
    $y$ axis.

    \begin{equation}
      \vectorbold{\hat{n}} \cdot \vectorbold{\hat{y}} = 0
    \end{equation}

    \begin{equation}
      \norm{ \vectorbold{\hat{n}} \cdot \vectorbold{\hat{x}}}^2
      + \norm{\vectorbold{\hat{n}} \cdot \vectorbold{\hat{z}}}^2
      = 1
    \end{equation}

    Since we're using $S_x$ as our measurement basis, let's pull up
    those definitions also:

    \begin{equation*}
      \tag{1.110a}
      \begin{split}
        \ket{x\pm} &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} [\ket + \pm \ket -]\\
        &=> \ket{x+} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} [\ket + + \ket -]
      \end{split}
    \end{equation*}

    \begin{equation*}
      \tag{1.111a}
      S_x = \frac{\hbar}{2} [\ket - \bra + + \ket + \bra - ]
    \end{equation*}

    Referencing our axis diagram (figure \qref{spinAxes}) in the QRF,
    we see that the problem describes an azimuth angle ($\alpha$) of
    $0$ and an elevation-complement angle ($\beta$) of $\gamma$.
    Applying our result from problem \ref{1.11:theQuestion}
    (\ref{1.11:spinAngle}/\qref{spinAngle}):

    \begin{equation}
      \inlabel{ket}
      \begin{split}
        \ket{\alpha_+}
        &= \cos{\frac{\beta}{2}} \ket + + e^{-i\alpha}\sin{\frac{\beta}{2}} \ket -\\
        &= \cos{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \ket + + \sin{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \ket -\\
      \end{split}
    \end{equation}

    Evaluating our probability:
    \begin{equation}
      \begin{split}
        P(x+)
        &= \abs{\braket{x+}{\alpha_+}\right}^2\\
        &= \abs{\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}
        (\bra + + \bra -)
        (\cos{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \ket + + \sin{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \ket -)}^2\\
        &= \frac{1}{2}\abs{
        \cos{\frac{\gamma}{2}}\braket +
        + \sin{\frac{\gamma}{2}}\braket -}^2\\
        &= \frac{1}{2}\abs{
        \cos{\frac{\gamma}{2}} + \sin{\frac{\gamma}{2}}
        }^2\\
        &= \frac{1}{2}\abs{
        (\cos^2{\frac{\gamma}{2}} + \sin^2{\frac{\gamma}{2}})
        + 2\cos{\frac{\gamma}{2}}\sin{\frac{\gamma}{2}}
        }\\
        &= \frac{1}{2}(1 + \sin{\gamma})\\
      \end{split}
    \end{equation}

    Obviously we can evaluate the general solution, but what fun is
    that?  

    \begin{equation}
      \begin{alignedat}{2}
      %
      &&\braket{x+}{\alpha_+} &=
      \bra{x+}\left(\cos{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \ket +
      + \sin{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \ket -\right)\\
      %
      \gamma &= 0 \quad &
      \braket{x+}{\alpha_+} &=
      \bra{x+}(\cos{0} \ket + + \sin{0} \ket -)\\
      & & &=
      \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} (\bra + + \bra -)(\cos{0} \ket + + \sin{0} \ket -)\\
      %
      & & &=
      \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\\
      %
      \gamma &= \frac{\pi}{2} \quad &
      \braket{x+}{\alpha_+} &=
      \bra{x+}(\cos{\frac{\pi}{4}} \ket + + \sin{\frac{\pi}{4}} \ket -)\\
      & & &=
      \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\bra + + \bra -)\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket + + \ket -)\\
      & & &=
      \frac{1}{2}(\braket + + \braket -)\\
      & & &=
      1\\
      %
      \gamma &= \pi \quad &
      \braket{x+}{\alpha_+} &=
      \bra{x+}\left(\cos{\frac{\pi}{2}} \ket +
      + \sin{\frac{\pi}{2}} \ket -\right)\\
      & & &=
      \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\bra + + \bra -)\left(\cos{\frac{\pi}{2}} \ket +
      + \sin{\frac{\pi}{2}} \ket -\right)\\
      & & &=
      \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\\
      %
    \end{alignedat}
  \end{equation}

  Translating those magnitudes into probabilities, we see that when we
  are aligned with $x+$, $P(\frac{\hbar}{2}) = 1$ whereas when we are
  perpendicular to it, our probability is $\frac 1 2$.  When we're
  perpendicular, then we have no information about which way it'll go
  (either $\frac \hbar 2$ or $- \frac \hbar 2$) so we expect a
  probability of $\frac 1 2$.

  \begin{equation}
    \begin{alignedat}{2}
      P(x+;\gamma&=0) & &= \frac 1 2\\
      P(x+;\gamma&=\frac{\pi}{2}) & &= 1\\
      P(x+;\gamma&=\pi) & &= \frac 1 2\\
    \end{alignedat}
  \end{equation}

  \end{answerpart}

  \begin{answerpart}{Dispersion}

    Recalling equation 1.144:
    \begin{equation*}
      \tag{1.144}
      \expval{(\Delta A)^2}
      = \expval{(A^2 - 2A\expval{A} + \expval{A}^2)}
      = \expval{A^2} - \expval{A}^2
    \end{equation*}

    \begin{equation}
      \begin{split}
        \expval{S_x}
        &= \mel{\alpha_+}{S_x}{\alpha_+}\\
      \end{split}
    \end{equation}

    Recalling equation \inref{ket}:

    \begin{equation}
      \ket{\alpha_+}
      = \cos{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \ket +
      + \sin{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \ket -\\
    \end{equation}
    
    Recalling equation 1.111a from the book:
    \begin{equation*}
      \tag{1.111a}
      S_x = \frac{\hbar}{2} [\ket - \bra + + \ket + \bra - ]
    \end{equation*}

    \begin{equation}
      \begin{split}
        \expval{S_x}
        &= \mel{\alpha_+}{S_x}{\alpha_+}\\
        &= (\cos{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \bra + + \sin{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \bra -)
        (\frac{\hbar}{2} [\ket - \bra + + \ket + \bra - ])
        (\cos{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \ket + + \sin{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \ket -)\\
        &= \frac{\hbar}{2}
        (\cos{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \bra + + \sin{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \bra -)
        (\sin{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \ket + + \cos{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \ket -)\\
        &= \frac{\hbar}{2}
        (\cos{\frac{\gamma}{2}}\sin{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \braket +
        \sin{\frac{\gamma}{2}}\cos{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \braket -)\\
        &= \frac{\hbar}{2} (2\cos{\frac{\gamma}{2}}\sin{\frac{\gamma}{2}})\\
        &= \frac{\hbar}{2}\sin{\gamma}\\
      \end{split}
    \end{equation}

    There are two ways to do this one, and it's worth going through
    both.  First, let's just grind it out:
    
    \begin{equation}
      \begin{split}
        \expval{S_x^2}
        &= \mel{\alpha_+}{S_x^2}{\alpha_+}\\
        &= (\cos{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \bra + + \sin{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \bra -)
        (\frac{\hbar}{2} [\ket - \bra + + \ket + \bra - ])        
        (\frac{\hbar}{2} [\ket - \bra + + \ket + \bra - ])
        (\cos{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \ket + + \sin{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \ket -)\\
        &= \frac{\hbar^2}{4}
        (\sin{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \bra + + \cos{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \bra -)
        (\sin{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \ket + + \cos{\frac{\gamma}{2}} \ket -)\\
        &= \frac{\hbar^2}{4}
        (\sin^2{\frac{\gamma}{2}}\braket{+}
        + \cos^2{\frac{\gamma}{2}}\braket{-})\\
        &= \frac{\hbar^2}{4}
      \end{split}
    \end{equation}

    Next, we'll make sue of the identity in \ref{1.10:sxSquared}, we
    can rewrite our equation:

    \begin{equation}
      \begin{split}
        \expval{S_x^2}
        &= \mel{\alpha_+}{S_x^2}{\alpha_+}\\
        &= \mel{\alpha_+}{\frac{\hbar^2}{4}I}{\alpha_+}\\
        &= \frac{\hbar^2}{4}\braket{\alpha_+}\\
        &= \frac{\hbar^2}{4}\\
      \end{split}
    \end{equation}

    Putting it all together:

    \begin{equation}
      \begin{split}
        \expval{(S_x - \expval{S_x})^2}
        &= \expval{S_x^2} - \expval{S_x}^2\\
        &= \frac{\hbar^2}{4} - \frac{\hbar^2}{4}\sin^2{\gamma}\\
        &= \frac{\hbar^2}{4}(1 - \sin^2{\gamma})\\
      \end{split}
    \end{equation}

    Going back and referencing our axes (figure \qref{spinAxes}), we
    see that when $\vectorbold{\hat{n}}$ is aligned with the $x$ axis,
    the dispersion is $0$:

    \begin{equation}
      \begin{split}
        \expval{(S_x - \expval{S_x})^2}
        &= \frac{\hbar^2}{4}(1 - \sin^2{\gamma})\\
        &= \frac{\hbar^2}{4}(1 - \sin^2{\frac \pi 2})\\
        &= \frac{\hbar^2}{4}(1 - 1)\\
        &= 0\\
      \end{split}
    \end{equation}

    Alternatively, when we're perpendicular to our operator's axis, we
    find that the dispersion is maximized at $\frac{\hbar^2}{4}$:
    
    \begin{equation}
      \begin{split}
        \expval{(S_x - \expval{S_x})^2}
        &= \frac{\hbar^2}{4}(1 - \sin^2{\gamma})\\
        &= \frac{\hbar^2}{4}(1 - \sin^2{0})\\
        &= \frac{\hbar^2}{4}(1 - 0)\\
        &= \frac{\hbar^2}{4}\\
      \end{split}
    \end{equation}

    In classical statistical theory, the dispersion is a measure of
    how diverse the data are.  By amplifying small differences between
    the input values it tells us how concentrated the data are.  As we
    saw in problem \ref{1.3:theProblem}, the quantum mechanical view
    of the dispersion is more related to what Heisenberg may have
    called the indeterminacy of the system or about how much
    information (a la Claude Shannon) the $S_x$ operator provides when
    states are prepared along the $\vectorbold{\hat{n}}$ axis.  When
    the state is prepared along the $x$ axis, then observations don't
    provide any information (it starts as $x$, it ends as $x$, you
    don't change it).  However, when you start aligning it with other
    axes, you start introducing variability in the outcome, and you
    start seeing the dispersion increase.
  \end{answerpart}

\end{answer}
