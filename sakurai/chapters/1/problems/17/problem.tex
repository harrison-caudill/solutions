\begin{question}

  Let $A$ and $B$ be observables. Suppose the simultaneous eigenkets
  of $A$ and $B$ $\left\{\ket{a',b'}\right\}$ form a complete
  orthonormal set of base kets. Can we always conclude that

  \begin{equation*}
    [A, B] = 0?
  \end{equation*}

  If your answer is yes, prove the assertion. If your answer is no,
  give a counterexample.

\end{question}

\begin{purpose}
  I guess to make sure you have a pulse?
\end{purpose}

\begin{answer}
  
  Because, and I can't emphasize this enough, I'm lazy and don't want
  to type $\left\{\ket{a',b'}\right\}$ a bunch, I'm going to redefine
  this

  \begin{equation}
    \text{let }\left\{\ket{a',b'}\right\} \equiv \left\{\ket{c_i}\right\}
  \end{equation}
  
  When in doubt, multiply by $1$!

  \begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
      A &= A1\\
      &= \sum_{i}A\dyad{c_i}\\
      &= \sum_{i}a_i\dyad{c_i}\\
      &= \sum_{i}a_i\Lambda_i\\
    \end{split}
  \end{equation}

  \begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
      B &= B1\\
      &= \sum_{i}B\dyad{c_i}\\
      &= \sum_{i}b_i\dyad{c_i}\\
      &= \sum_{i}b_i\Lambda_i\\
    \end{split}
  \end{equation}

  We start with our definition of commutation

  \begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
      [A, B] &= AB - BA\\
    \end{split}
  \end{equation}

  Next, we work out one part of that:

  \begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
      AB
      &= \left(\sum_{i} a_i\Lambda_i\right)\left(\sum_{j} b_j\Lambda_j\right)\\
    \end{split}
  \end{equation}

  Since we know that $\Lambda_i\Lambda_j = \Lambda_i\delta_{ij}$, we can
  eliminate any cross-terms in that giant ugly mess and come back with:
  
  \begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
      AB
      &= \left(\sum_{i} a_i\Lambda_i\right)\left(\sum_{j} b_j\Lambda_j\right)\\
      &= \sum_{i} a_ib_i\Lambda_i\\
    \end{split}
  \end{equation}

  Since we can rearrange a constant anytime we please, let's elect to
  do so now

  \begin{equation}
    \inlabel{result}
    \begin{split}
      AB
      &= \left(\sum_{i} a_i\Lambda_i\right)\left(\sum_{j} b_j\Lambda_j\right)\\
      &= \sum_{i} a_ib_i\Lambda_i\\
      &= \sum_{i} b_ia_i\Lambda_i\\
      &= \left(\sum_{i} b_i\Lambda_i\right)\left(\sum_{j} a_j\Lambda_j\right)\\
      &= BA\qed
    \end{split}
  \end{equation}

\end{answer}
