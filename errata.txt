2. Overcomplicated Proof: Problem 1.19a

In Problem 1.19a, you spent 3–4 pages using projection operators and heavily
subscripted indices to prove that the energy eigenstates are degenerate. While
your logic eventually gets there, you missed the "standard" 3-line quantum
mechanics proof by contradiction, which is much more elegant:

Simpler Proof: Assume the energy eigenstate \ket{E} is non-degenerate. Because
[A_1, H] = 0, \ket{E} must also be an eigenstate of A_1 (let's say
A_1\ket{E} = a_1\ket{E}). Because [A_2, H] = 0, \ket{E} must also be an
eigenstate of A_2 (let's say A_2\ket{E} = a_2\ket{E}). If \ket{E} is a
simultaneous eigenstate of both, then:
[A_1, A_2]\ket{E} = (A_1 A_2 - A_2 A_1)\ket{E} = (a_1 a_2 - a_2 a_1)\ket{E} = 0.
But the problem gives us that [A_1, A_2] \neq 0. This is a contradiction.
Therefore, our assumption is false, and \ket{E} must be degenerate.

4. Circular Logic: Problem 1.36a

To prove that \mel{p'}{x}{\alpha} = i\hbar \pdv{}{p'}\braket{p'}{\alpha}, you
assumed the form of the Gaussian wave packet \exp(\frac{ip'x'}{\hbar}) and
worked backward. While this shows it works for a specific function, it doesn't
prove it in the general abstract case.

  - A more rigorous approach: Use the basic commutator [x, p] = i\hbar. Take the
    matrix element between momentum eigenstates:
    \mel{p'}{[x, p]}{p''} = i\hbar \braket{p'}{p''}. Expand the commutator:
    \mel{p'}{x p - p x}{p''} = (p'' - p') \mel{p'}{x}{p''} = i\hbar \delta(p' - p'').
    From the properties of the derivative of a delta function,
    (p'' - p') \mel{p'}{x}{p''} = i\hbar \delta(p' - p'') implies that
    \mel{p'}{x}{p''} = -i\hbar \pdv{}{p'} \delta(p' - p''). If you insert this
    into the integral \int dp'' \mel{p'}{x}{p''} \braket{p''}{\alpha}, you get
    the general result i\hbar \pdv{}{p'}\braket{p'}{\alpha} without needing to
    assume the shape of the wave function!
